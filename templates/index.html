Your thought partner for the hard academic moments

This coach is here for neurodivergent college students navigating assignments, energy, and everything in between. It is not a tutor, not a therapist, and not a generic productivity app. It is a collaborative thought partner — warm, specific, and built around how you actually work.

  • 🗂
    Decode confusing situations
    Assignment prompts, rubric language, professor feedback, and presentations you are dreading.
  • 🧭
    Apply what you know about yourself
    Take your self-knowledge and turn it into one concrete action for the specific challenge in front of you right now.
  • 🛠
    Get strategies that fit you
    Real, non-generic options based on your environment, energy, and time — not a five-step productivity plan.
  • 🌿
    Check in when you're running low
    Recognise masking fatigue and burnout before it becomes a crisis. Rest is always a legitimate option here.

The coach asks questions rather than lectures. It will never diagnose you, label you, or tell you what you should be doing. If you are in crisis at any point, it will provide campus counseling resources and 988 immediately.

What a session looks like

You
I have a paper due Friday and I keep re-reading the prompt but I genuinely do not know what my professor wants. It says "critically analyze the systemic factors" and I don't know what that means.
Coach
That phrase trips a lot of people up. "Critically analyze" in academic writing usually means: don't just describe what happened — explain why it happened, who benefited, and what kept it in place. "Systemic factors" means look beyond individual choices and point to structures: policies, institutions, historical patterns. So the assignment is probably asking: pick a situation, and argue what larger forces caused or sustained it — not just what individuals did. Does that reframe land, or does something about the prompt still feel off?
You
That actually helps a lot. So I don't need to summarize the topic, I need to argue something about it?
Coach
Exactly. You need a claim — something like "X persists because of Y and Z" — and then use evidence to back that up. The summary is just context, not the paper itself.
You
I have a group presentation next week. I know I do better when I can prepare alone first, but group work never lets me do that. I don't know how to make this work for me.
Coach
That self-knowledge is actually really useful here. You don't need the group to change — you just need to build your solo prep time in before the group sessions, not alongside them. Here's one adjusted approach: before your next group meeting, spend 20–30 minutes alone writing down what you think the presentation should cover and what your strongest contribution would be. Bring that as a draft, not a question. You show up with something concrete instead of waiting to be assigned a role. Does that feel doable, or is there a constraint that makes it harder?
You
That actually sounds manageable. I always wait to see what everyone else does and then feel stuck.
Coach
Coming in with your own prep flips that. You're not taking over — you're just arriving ready.
You
I am so tired. I have been pushing through everything for weeks and I don't know how much longer I can keep doing this.
Coach
That sounds like more than a rough week — it sounds like you've been holding a lot for a long time without a real break. You don't have to justify how tired you are. Weeks of pushing through without recovery catches up, and what you're describing is a real signal worth listening to. Is there anything that has to happen in the next 48 hours, or is there any room at all to put something down?
You
I have one lab report due tomorrow but after that I have two days before anything else is due.
Coach
Get the lab report done, and then let those two days actually be rest. Not productive rest, not catching up on readings. Rest. Your brain needs it to function. What does actual rest look like for you? Not what you think you should do — what actually recharges you.

Filling out your profile

The About You panel (the tab on the right side of the chat screen) lets you tell the coach a bit about yourself before your first message. Everything is optional — share only what feels useful.

  • 👤
    Name or nickname
    The coach uses it naturally in responses. You can use any name you are comfortable with.
  • 🔢
    Age
    Helps calibrate the tone and context of suggestions.
  • 🧩
    What applies to you
    Select any neurodivergent conditions that feel relevant. This is never used to label or define you — only to help the coach understand your context better.
  • 📋
    A bit about your situation
    Year in school, major, what your schedule is typically like. Even a sentence helps.
  • ⚡
    What tends to stress you out
    Vague instructions, group projects, not knowing where to start — anything that makes things harder. The coach uses this to avoid making things worse.
